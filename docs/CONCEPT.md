# Concept

## The premise

The legend says only the true king can pull the sword from the stone.

Arthur grabs the hilt. He pulls. Nothing.

He braces, heaves with everything he has — and the **sword stays put while the
stone tears loose from the ground**. Now he's standing there holding a
boulder-sized rock with a sword sticking out of it, the whole kingdom watching.

He did not pass the test. He **broke the test**.

And honestly? Swinging a stone the size of a cart turns out to be a more
convincing argument than any sword. He keeps it.

## The joke, and why it isn't *just* a joke

> *What if the chosen one failed the test, but broke the testing system instead?*

The comedy is the hook. The **mechanic** is the point. The stone-sword is a
perfect vehicle for a specific kind of combat feel: the heavy, committed, "oh no
I've started the swing and I can't stop it now" weapon. The premise gives us a
*reason* for that weight that the player instantly understands — no tutorial
needed. You see a man holding a rock; you already know it's going to be slow.

## Tone

Playful, but not sloppy. Think:

- **mythological parody** — the framing
- **physics combat** — the gameplay
- **creative-engineering prototype** — the craft
- **game-jam energy** — the scope

It should read as a serious prototype with a funny premise, **not** a meme
project. The humour comes from the *situation* and the *physics*, not from
winking at the camera.

## The one sentence that governs every decision

> **Arthur is powerful because he lifted the whole stone — but the stone also
> makes him slow, vulnerable, and hard to control.**

If a feature doesn't sharpen that trade-off, it waits.

## Central tensions to explore

- power ↔ control
- weight ↔ mobility
- impact ↔ recovery time
- momentum ↔ precision
- stamina cost ↔ damage output

These aren't separate features — they're five faces of the same coin. The
prototype tunes them until the coin feels good to flip.
